The Cl0p ransomware gang has yet again changed its extortion strategy
The Cl0p ransomware group has once again altered its approach to increase pressure on victims impacted by the MOVEit hack. In a span of two weeks, the gang shifted its tactics to exploit the situation. Beginning on May 27, the group targeted 597 organizations through the exploitation of a zero-day vulnerability in the MOVEit secure transfer file platform. Subsequently, on June 14, they escalated their extortion efforts by listing victims' names on their Tor data leak site. However, constraints with Tor sites prompted the gang to continuously adapt their methods for disseminating the stolen data from the MOVEit breach. In their most recent move, the notorious gang has turned to torrent sites for more efficient data leaks. The process involves offering instructions for downloading the leaked information through a new Tor site established by the gang. Security researcher Dominic Alvieri noted that torrents have been generated for 20 victims, including notable names such as Aon, K&L Gates, Putnam, Delaware Life, Zurich Brazil, and Heidelberg. By July 23, the Cl0p gang had set up clearweb sites for each victim, streamlining the extortion process. This approach heightened urgency among employees, executives, and partners, compelling organizations to meet ransom demands upon discovering their compromised data on the website. However, the use of clearweb sites was short-lived, as law enforcement intervened and took these sites offline without the gang's awareness. Torrent sites pose a challenge for law enforcement due to their decentralized nature. The original seeder can be replaced with a new device, allowing the stolen data to be consistently seeded. Furthermore, unlike Tor data leak sites that suffer from slow download speeds, torrents boast quicker transfer rates by utilizing peer-to-peer sharing among users. Coveware, an industry observer, projected that Cl0p stands to amass significant revenue, estimated between $75 million and $100 million, solely from extorting victims affected by the MOVEit data breach. Employing such strategies empowers the gang to exert additional pressure on victims, compelling them to meet ransom demands. This evolution in tactics highlights the gang's adaptability and resourcefulness in the pursuit of illicit gains.
Bypass Google Play Store
Threat actors are employing a technique called versioning to evade Google Play Store's malware detection and target Android users, according to the Google Cybersecurity Action Team (GCAT) in its August 2023 Threat Horizons Report shared with The Hacker News. Versioning is not a new tactic but remains difficult to detect. In this method, developers release an initial app version on the Play Store, which passes Google's pre-publication checks. However, the app is later updated with a malware component, making it a sneaky backdoor. Recently, ESET discovered a seemingly harmless screen recording app named "iRecorder - Screen Recorder" that turned malicious after almost a year on the Play Store. Malicious changes were introduced quietly to spy on users. Another example is the SharkBot malware, which repeatedly appeared on the Play Store disguised as security and utility apps. SharkBot is a financial trojan used to initiate unauthorized money transfers via the Automated Transfer Service (ATS) protocol. To execute this attack, dropper applications with reduced functionality are uploaded to the Play Store, attracting less attention. Once installed, these apps download the full version of the malware. The findings coincide with ThreatFabric's revelation that malware creators exploit an Android bug to make malicious apps appear benign. They achieve this by "corrupting components of an app" while keeping the entire app valid. To mitigate risks, Android users are advised to download apps only from trusted sources and enable Google Play Protect to receive notifications about potentially harmful apps (PHAs) on their devices. Additionally, in an enterprise environment, implementing defense-in-depth principles such as limiting app installation sources to trusted platforms like Google Play and using mobile device management (MDM) platforms for corporate devices is crucial.
Reptile Rootkit: Advanced Linux Malware Targeting South Korean Systems
Threat actors are utilizing an open-source rootkit named Reptile to target Linux systems in South Korea. Rather than just offering concealment capabilities, Reptile goes a step further by providing a reverse shell, which grants attackers easy control over compromised systems. The AhnLab Security Emergency Response Center (ASEC) reported on this, stating that Reptile enables threat actors to establish connections to their command and control (C&C) server through a method called port knocking. This involves the malware opening a specific port on an infected system and waiting for a "magic packet" to be sent by the attacker. Once received, this packet contains the C&C server address, and a reverse shell connects to it. A rootkit is malicious software designed to grant unauthorized, high-level access to a system while hiding its presence. Reptile has been utilized in at least four separate campaigns since 2022. The initial use of this rootkit was identified by Trend Micro in May 2022, associated with an intrusion set called Earth Berberoka, which used Reptile to obscure connections and processes linked to a cross-platform Python trojan known as Pupy RAT. This activity was directed at gambling sites in China. In March 2023, Mandiant, owned by Google, disclosed an attack by a suspected China-linked threat actor called UNC3886. This actor utilized zero-day vulnerabilities in Fortinet appliances to deploy custom implants, along with Reptile. Another incident in the same month revealed a Chinese hacking group's employment of a Linux malware named Mélofée, based on Reptile. Additionally, Microsoft discovered a cryptojacking operation in June 2023 that employed Reptile to mask its child processes, files, and content using a shell script backdoor. Further analysis of Reptile indicates the use of a loader that employs a tool named kmatryoshka. This loader decrypts and loads the rootkit's kernel module into memory, subsequently opening a specific port and waiting for an attacker to send a magic packet via protocols such as TCP, UDP, or ICMP. This magic packet contains the C&C server address, which facilitates the connection of a reverse shell to the C&C server. It's noteworthy that the use of magic packets to trigger malicious activity was previously observed in another rootkit named Syslogk, as documented by Avast the previous year. ASEC, the South Korean cybersecurity firm, noted a case involving Reptile within the country, which shared tactical similarities with the Mélofée attack. To sum up, Reptile is a Linux kernel mode rootkit that not only conceals files, directories, processes, and network communications but also provides a reverse shell, potentially allowing threat actors to hijack systems where Reptile is present. This news arrives shortly after Trend Micro identified new variants of another evasive Linux backdoor, BPFDoor, which was employed by a Chinese threat actor known as Red Menshen. These new variants revealed active development and a successful track record for the malware.
Meow Attack Campaign Evolves to Target Jupyter Notebooks
Aquasec researchers recently discovered a concerning cyber threat called the "Meow" attack campaign. This ongoing automated attack is specifically focusing on vulnerable Jupyter notebooks that are publicly accessible. The attackers have managed to compromise numerous databases on the internet using this method. Their strategy involves identifying misconfigured Jupyter Notebook instances, which they seem to have located using the Shodan search tool. Although the investigators are still unraveling the intricacies of the attackers' infrastructure, they have identified a total of 1,283 unique IP addresses that have been targeted. The attackers followed a systematic approach: first, they gained access to the victim's system and used a dash shell to gather essential information, such as user IDs, processor types, architecture details, and operating system information, including name and version. Subsequently, the attackers downloaded a malicious script from a shared file server. This script was then executed on the compromised Jupyter notebook after ensuring that the required Python packages were in place. What makes this situation particularly interesting is the attackers' utilization of Python scripts to focus on databases, displaying an unconventional mode of operation. Within the discovered attacks, a specific script named 'foo' was identified across 1,354 IP addresses. This script was directed at databases employing Elasticsearch and MongoDB. Additionally, AquaSec's experts observed another script called 'bar' targeting unsecured Hadoop clusters. Since these attacks are automated and exploit well-known vulnerabilities to target exposed internet-facing databases, they pose a global threat and can inflict substantial harm on affected companies. An earlier instance of Meow attacks, in 2021, led to significant data loss. Notably, Western Digital experienced data erasure amounting to petabytes, while a well-known cosmetic brand, Yves Rocher, had millions of records wiped out. In total, these criminals have erased data from over 4,000 databases, which encompass platforms like Cassandra, CouchDB, Redis, Hadoop, Jenkins, and Apache ZooKeeper. The reappearance of Meow attacks after a hiatus of over a year emphasizes the importance of organizations regularly examining their databases for security vulnerabilities. It is crucial to implement measures such as Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) across all devices that store sensitive organizational data. These steps are necessary to safeguard against potential threats and data breaches that could arise from these automated attacks.
VMware ESXi Servers Face New Threat from Abyss Locker
MalwareHunterTeam recently uncovered a fresh variant of the Abyss Locker ransomware, specifically engineered to target Linux-based VMware ESXi servers. This variant is part of the broader Abyss ransomware family, which has been operational since 2019 and has targeted diverse platforms and systems. The Linux iteration of Abyss Locker employs advanced attack techniques to illicitly infiltrate VMware ESXi servers. It capitalizes on SSH brute force attacks to exploit vulnerable or compromised credentials and gain unauthorized entry into the system. Once inside, the ransomware proceeds to encrypt virtual machines, effectively locking them and rendering them inaccessible and unusable. Following the encryption process, the malicious actors responsible for the ransomware release ransom notes, demanding payment in cryptocurrency, primarily Bitcoin, in exchange for the decryption key. Since 2019, Abyss Locker has been active in the cybercrime landscape, showcasing a growing trend of ransomware attacks aimed at Linux-based systems. This is noteworthy since Linux systems were once considered less susceptible to such assaults. The ransomware operators assert that they have exfiltrated data ranging from 35GB to 700GB from various companies. Researchers have detected similarities between the Abyss Locker Linux encryptor and the HelloKitty ransomware. According to Michael Gillespie of Bleeping Computer, the Abyss Locker Linux encryptor appears to be derived from HelloKitty, with the former utilizing ChaCha encryption instead. In contrast, HelloKitty typically employs a combination of AES-256 and RSA-2048, or even NTRU+AES-128. Currently, it remains unclear whether this is a rebranding of the HelloKitty operation or if another ransomware group has gained access to the encryptor's source code. The discovery of the Linux version of Abyss Locker underscores the evolving nature of ransomware attacks. Furthermore, the Abyss Locker operators exhibit considerable skill and a history of launching targeted assaults on high-value assets.
Nitrogen Malvertising - Sneaky Malware in Search Ads
The recently detected malvertising campaign, named Nitrogen, has been found exploiting Google Search and Bing ads to target users seeking IT tools. This campaign tricks users into downloading contaminated installers containing trojans. Its primary goal seems to be infiltrating enterprise networks, potentially setting the stage for future ransomware attacks. The Nitrogen campaign mainly focuses on technology and non-profit organizations in North America. It operates by disguising itself as installers for popular software like AnyDesk, Cisco AnyConnect VPN, TreeSize Free, and WinSCP. When users search for these applications, they encounter ads endorsing the software they are searching for. Upon clicking the provided link, users are redirected to compromised WordPress hosting pages imitating the genuine software download sites for the specific application they were looking for. Throughout the infection process, the threat actors employ less common techniques such as export forwarding and DLL sideloading to conceal their malicious activities and complicate analysis. Using Python scripts, they initiate a Meterpreter reverse TCP shell, allowing them to remotely execute code on compromised systems. There are instances where the attackers have engaged in hands-on actions after running the Meterpreter script on targeted systems. They manually input commands to acquire additional ZIP files and Python 3 environments. The latter is necessary for running Cobalt Strike in memory, as the NitrogenStager lacks the ability to execute Python scripts. Trend Micro's earlier report suggests that this attack chain has resulted in the deployment of the BlackCat ransomware in at least one documented case. This shows the sophistication and danger of the Nitrogen campaign.
Introducing FraudGPT
In the aftermath of WormGPT's success, threat actors have now unleashed another AI-powered cybercrime tool named FraudGPT. This malicious AI bot is being actively promoted on various dark web marketplaces and Telegram channels, boasting capabilities such as crafting spear-phishing emails, generating cracking tools, and facilitating carding activities. The individual behind this threat goes by the alias CanadianKingpin and is promoting themselves as a verified vendor on several underground dark web marketplaces, including EMPIRE, WHM, TORREZ, WORLD, ALPHABAY, and VERSUS. Their promotion of FraudGPT has been ongoing since at least July 22, offering subscription plans at $200 per month, $1,000 for six months, and $1,700 for a year. According to their claims, FraudGPT can be employed to create malicious code, develop undetectable malware, and identify leaks and vulnerabilities. Furthermore, it has garnered over 3,000 confirmed sales and reviews. As of now, the specific Large Language Model (LLM) used to develop this system remains undisclosed. WormGPT, another AI tool, is designed to facilitate sophisticated phishing and BEC attacks. It automates the creation of highly convincing fake emails, personalized for individual recipients, significantly increasing the success rate of these attacks. Additionally, it boasts extensive character support, chat memory retention, and code formatting capabilities. The existence of WormGPT and FraudGPT raises significant concerns, particularly because AI models like OpenAI ChatGPT and Google Bard have taken measures to prevent their misuse in deceptive emails and malicious code. The use of generative AI in these attacks makes them more accessible to a wider range of cybercriminals. These tools not only bolster the Phishing-as-a-Service (PhaaS) model but also provide a platform for inexperienced individuals seeking to carry out large-scale and persuasive phishing and BEC attacks. In light of these developments, organizations must adopt a proactive approach to safeguard their data, systems, and customers. Implementing robust security measures and staying vigilant against evolving threats can help businesses strengthen their resilience against AI-driven attacks and protect themselves from potential financial and reputational damages.
Ubuntu users vulnerable
Recently, two vulnerabilities were discovered in the Ubuntu Linux kernel, which could potentially allo23-32629 and CVE-2023-2640. The first one, CVE-2023-2640, is a high-severity vulnerability with a CVSS v3 scorw unprivileged local users to gain elevated privileges. These vulnerabilities are tracked as CVE-20e of 7.8. It is caused by inadequate permission checks, enabling a local attacker to gain elevated privileges. The second vulnerability, CVE-2023-32629, has a medium-severity with a CVSS v3 score of 5.4. It is related to the Linux kernel memory management subsystem and involves a race condition when accessing VMAs, leading to a use-after-free scenario. A local attacker can exploit this flaw to perform arbitrary code execution. The root cause of these vulnerabilities was the introduction of custom changes to the OverlayFS module in Ubuntu in 2018. The Linux kernel project later made modifications to the module in 2019 and 2022, creating conflicts with Ubuntu's changes. When the widespread distribution adopted the new code containing these changes, it led to the introduction of the two vulnerabilities. The risk of exploitation is high, as proof-of-concept exploits for these vulnerabilities have been publicly available for a significant period. The vulnerabilities are unique to Ubuntu kernels due to their specific changes to the OverlayFS module. Ubuntu has addressed these vulnerabilities in the latest version of the Ubuntu Linux kernel and released a security bulletin about the issues. Alongside these two flaws, six other vulnerabilities were also fixed. Users are urged to update their systems via the package manager to ensure they receive the necessary updates and post-install configurations. A system reboot is required for the Linux kernel update to take effect. It is crucial to note that only Ubuntu and other Linux distributions that use custom modifications of the OverlayFS module are impacted. Distributions that haven't made such changes should be safe from these specific vulnerabilities. As of now, this information serves as a warning for Ubuntu users to promptly update their systems to protect against potential exploitation of these vulnerabilities.
